## Why This Lesson Exists

Before you can read a geopolitical event and know what it means for markets, you need a framework. Not intuition — a framework. The difference is this: intuition says "war is bad for stocks." A framework says "this type of war, between these types of actors, in this type of resource geography, at this point in the global order, has these specific effects on these specific asset classes — and here is the historical precedent."

The three foundational frameworks are **Realism**, **Liberalism**, and **Constructivism**. Every serious geopolitical analyst uses some combination of these, usually without naming them. By naming them and understanding their logic, you will see what others are doing intuitively — and do it deliberately.

---

## Part I: Realism — The World as Power Competition

**The core claim:** States are the primary actors in international politics. They operate in an anarchic system — there is no world government, no ultimate enforcer. In this system, states pursue power and security above all else. Trust is scarce. Cooperation is fragile. Conflict is the default state of nature.

The intellectual fathers: **Thucydides** (the Melian Dialogue, 416 BC), **Thomas Hobbes** (the state of nature), **Hans Morgenthau** (Politics Among Nations, 1948), and more recently **John Mearsheimer** (offensive realism — states don't just want enough power, they want as much as possible).

**The key insight for investors:** Realism predicts that great powers will always compete for influence over critical resources, trade routes, and strategic geography — regardless of trade relationships, international institutions, or ideological alignment. Economic interdependence does not prevent conflict; it just raises the cost. When cost calculations shift (as they did with China-US decoupling), conflict becomes more likely, not less.

**Historical example:** The Thucydides Trap. When a rising power threatens to displace a ruling power, war is the historical norm. Thucydides described it in 431 BC: "It was the rise of Athens and the fear that this inspired in Sparta that made war inevitable." Mearsheimer counts 16 cases of rising power challenging ruling power in the last 500 years. War occurred in 12 of them.

*Ask yourself:* China's GDP is projected to surpass the US by 2030. The US controls the global reserve currency, most major sea lanes, and the dominant military alliance system. What does realism predict? Not "maybe conflict." It predicts that conflict — trade, technological, potentially military — is structurally overdetermined. The only question is form and timing.

**Investment translation:** Realism tells you to look for resource competition, strategic chokepoints, and great-power rivalry as durable, multi-decade themes — not events. Defense spending, energy independence, critical mineral sovereignty, and semiconductor nationalism are all realist outputs. They do not reverse when a new president is elected or a trade deal is signed.

## Part II: Liberalism — The World as Interdependence

**The core claim:** States are not the only actors. International institutions, multinational corporations, NGOs, and democratic norms also shape behavior. Economic interdependence creates mutual interests in stability. Democracies rarely go to war with each other. International law and institutions can constrain state behavior.

The intellectual fathers: **Immanuel Kant** (Perpetual Peace, 1795), **Woodrow Wilson** (the League of Nations), **Robert Keohane and Joseph Nye** (complex interdependence, 1977).

**The key insight for investors:** Liberalism underpins the entire post-WWII investment thesis. The Bretton Woods system, the WTO, the EU, SWIFT, dollar hegemony — these are all liberal constructions. They created the stable, rules-based environment in which modern capital markets operate. When they work, they suppress geopolitical risk premia across all asset classes. When they erode, those premia return.

**Historical example:** The post-1945 liberal order. After WWII, the US constructed a system of institutions (UN, IMF, World Bank, GATT/WTO) designed to make war between major powers too economically costly. For 70 years, this largely worked. The integration of China into the WTO in 2001 was the high-water mark of liberal optimism — the belief that trade would liberalize China politically. It didn't. That failure is now being priced into markets in the form of decoupling, tariffs, and export controls.

*Ask yourself:* The liberal order is fraying — but it has not collapsed. SWIFT still processes most global transactions. The dollar is still the reserve currency. The WTO still exists. What is the investment implication of a world that is neither fully liberal nor fully realist — a fragmented, multipolar system where some institutions function and others don't?

**Investment translation:** Liberalism tells you to watch institutional integrity as a market signal. When institutions hold (WTO dispute resolution, IMF credibility, Fed independence), risk premia compress. When they erode (weaponization of SWIFT, tariff regimes that bypass WTO, political interference with central banks), risk premia expand. Right now, institutional erosion is accelerating — which is why gold, defense, and commodity exposure are all rational responses.

## Part III: Constructivism — The World as Perception

**The core claim:** International politics is not just about material power (realism) or institutions (liberalism). It is also shaped by ideas, identities, and perceptions. What states believe about each other — threat, ally, rival — shapes behavior as much as actual capabilities. Interests are not fixed; they are socially constructed.

The intellectual father: **Alexander Wendt** ("Anarchy is What States Make of It," 1992). The famous line: "500 British nuclear weapons are less threatening to the United States than 5 North Korean nuclear weapons" — not because of capability difference alone, but because of identity and perceived intent.

**The key insight for investors:** Constructivism explains why narratives matter in geopolitics — and in markets. A country's behavior changes when its identity changes (post-Soviet Russia deciding it was a "great power," China deciding it was a "civilization state" rather than a developing nation). Narrative shifts often precede policy shifts, which precede market shifts. If you can read the narrative before the policy moves, you have alpha.

**Historical example:** The US-China relationship from "strategic partner" (1990s-2000s) to "strategic competitor" (post-2017). This was not driven by a sudden change in material capabilities. China's GDP and military were growing throughout. What changed was the US narrative — and once the narrative shifted, the policy followed: export controls, tariffs, investment restrictions, semiconductor bans. The investors who anticipated the narrative shift (not just the policy announcement) had years of lead time.

*Ask yourself:* What narrative shifts are underway right now that haven't yet produced policy? The EU's framing of China as a "systemic rival." India's quiet repositioning as a strategic partner of the West. Saudi Arabia's de-dollarization experiments. These are constructivist signals — identity shifts that will eventually produce policy shifts, which will eventually produce market moves.

**Investment translation:** Constructivism tells you to read speeches, not just data. When Xi Jinping uses the phrase "the East is rising, the West is declining," that is not rhetoric — it is an identity claim with policy consequences. When Trump uses "America First," that is a reordering of identity that restructures every alliance. Track narrative shifts as leading indicators of policy shifts.

## Synthesis: How to Use All Three

No serious analyst uses only one framework. The practice is triangulation:

| Framework | Primary question | Investment signal |
|---|---|---|
| **Realism** | Who has power and who wants it? | Great-power competition, resource control, defense spending |
| **Liberalism** | Are institutions holding or eroding? | Risk premia, dollar credibility, multilateral trade |
| **Constructivism** | What do actors believe about themselves and others? | Narrative shifts as leading indicators |

**Applied to today:** The Iran war. Realism predicted it — the US and Iran have been in structural competition for regional dominance since 1979, and US-Israel strategic alignment made an eventual military confrontation probable. Liberalism explains why it shocked markets — the liberal expectation was that nuclear diplomacy (JCPOA) and economic pressure would contain Iran within institutional bounds. Constructivism explains the specific timing — Iran's leadership, having watched the US assassinate Soleimani and withdraw from the JCPOA, constructed an identity of existential threat that made deterrence insufficient.

The investor who held all three lenses simultaneously would have held energy and defense as structural longs (realism), treated diplomatic progress as temporary (liberalism eroding), and read Khamenei's 2024-2025 speeches as identity escalation signals (constructivism) — before the bombs fell.

## Market Connection

**The core investment thesis of this project** — that geopolitical events create systematic mispricings — is essentially a claim that most market participants are operating with one framework (usually an implicit liberal one: "institutions will hold, diplomacy will work, trade will continue") while reality operates across all three simultaneously.

The alpha opportunity is in the gap between the liberal baseline the market prices and the realist/constructivist reality that is unfolding.

The US-China relationship: a realist would say conflict is structurally inevitable; a liberal would point to $600B in annual trade as a deterrent; a constructivist would ask what each side believes the other to be. Which framework has the most predictive power for the next 5 years — and what does your answer imply for your portfolio?

3. Saudi Arabia is running simultaneous relationships with the US (security), China (trade), and Russia (OPEC+ coordination). Which framework best describes Saudi behavior — and what does it tell you about where the petrodollar system goes from here?
