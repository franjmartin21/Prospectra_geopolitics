## Core Concept: Energy as Geopolitical Weapon, Prize, and Curse

Energy is the master resource. Every other economic activity — manufacturing, transport, agriculture, warfare — runs on energy. This makes control of energy supply chains one of the most consequential determinants of geopolitical power.

But energy is not a simple asset. It is simultaneously:
1. **A strategic weapon** — states can cut off supply to coerce others
2. **A geopolitical prize** — the thing great powers fight over
3. **A curse** — the mechanism that often traps resource-rich states in dysfunction

Understanding all three dimensions is the framework.

---

## I. Energy as Strategic Weapon

The first time modern states learned to weaponize energy at scale was 1973. The Arab members of OPEC — furious at US support for Israel in the Yom Kippur War — imposed an oil embargo on the US, Netherlands, and others. The price of oil quadrupled in months. Western economies experienced inflation, recession, and long gasoline lines. The psychological trauma lasted decades and permanently altered US foreign policy calculations in the Middle East.

**The lesson was stark:** a state or coalition controlling a critical chokepoint in energy supply can impose enormous economic pain on the consumer without firing a shot.

The modern toolkit for energy warfare has expanded significantly:

- **Export cuts/embargoes** (OPEC 1973, Russia→Europe 2022)
- **Pipeline politics** — routing pipelines through or away from hostile states to create leverage or deny transit rents (Russia's Nord Stream strategy; Ukraine's role as a transit state)
- **Sanctions** — the US using the dollar system to deny Iran and Venezuela access to global oil markets
- **Strategic reserves** — the US SPR (Strategic Petroleum Reserve) exists precisely to blunt the coercive power of supply shocks

The Russian invasion of Ukraine in 2022 produced the most significant test of energy weaponization since 1973. Russia cut natural gas flows to Europe — weaponizing a dependency it had spent 20 years cultivating — expecting Europe to fracture and seek a separate peace. Europe's response (emergency LNG imports, demand reduction, accelerated renewable deployment, and ultimately Russian gas bans) revealed both the power and the limits of the energy weapon: it works until the target decides the cost of capitulation exceeds the cost of adjustment.

**Investment implication:** Energy weaponization creates price volatility windows. But more structurally, it accelerates the rewiring of energy supply chains — creating sustained demand for LNG infrastructure, pipeline rerouting, and alternative suppliers. This is a *multi-year* infrastructure investment thesis, not a trading call.

## II. Energy as Geopolitical Prize

Geography is not destiny in the abstract — it's destiny expressed through specific resources. The Persian Gulf contains ~48% of the world's proven oil reserves. The Strait of Hormuz, 21 miles wide at its narrowest point, is the transit point for ~20% of global oil supply. The entire strategic logic of US military presence in the Middle East for 50+ years — five carrier strike groups, multiple air bases, security guarantees to Saudi Arabia and the Gulf states — can be reduced to a single word: Hormuz.

Similarly:
- **The South China Sea** is contested because its sea lanes carry ~$3.4 trillion in annual trade and it contains potentially significant hydrocarbon reserves
- **Ukraine's location** matters partly because of gas transit routes and proximity to Russian energy infrastructure
- **Central Asia** (the "Great Game") has been contested for a century because of its hydrocarbon potential (Caspian basin) and transit geography
- **Arctic sovereignty disputes** are intensifying precisely because climate change is making Arctic hydrocarbon reserves and shipping routes commercially viable

**Critical minerals — the 21st century layer:**
The energy transition has added a new dimension to energy geopolitics. Solar panels, wind turbines, EVs, and grid storage all require critical minerals: lithium, cobalt, nickel, rare earth elements, copper. The geography of these minerals is highly concentrated:
- 60%+ of global cobalt production is in the DRC
- 55%+ of lithium production is in Australia and Chile
- 85%+ of rare earth processing is in China

China recognized this decades before the West and invested heavily in acquiring control of these supply chains — particularly in African mining assets. The West is now scrambling to build alternative supply chains. This is not a short-cycle story; it will play out over 15-20 years.

## III. The Resource Curse

Now return to the Saudi Arabia vs. Norway comparison from the opening question.

Economic theory predicts that resource windfalls should make countries richer and more stable. The empirical record is almost exactly the opposite. Resource-rich developing nations — Nigeria, Venezuela, Angola, DRC, Iraq — have systematically underperformed their resource-poor peers on economic development, institutional quality, and political stability. This is the *resource curse* (formally: the *paradox of plenty*).

The mechanisms are well-documented:

1. **Dutch Disease** — large resource export revenues cause the exchange rate to appreciate, making all non-resource sectors (manufacturing, agriculture, services) uncompetitive. The economy hollows out into a single-sector dependency. When commodity prices fall, there is nothing else.

2. **Rentier state dynamics** — when governments derive revenue from resource rents rather than taxation, they don't need to build the social contract (services in exchange for taxes) that creates political accountability. Citizens become subjects of the state's largesse rather than stakeholders demanding competent governance. Saudi Arabia spent decades subsidizing its population with oil money to buy political quiescence.

3. **Conflict financing** — resource revenues can finance insurgencies and civil wars that would otherwise collapse for lack of funding. Angola's UNITA, Sierra Leone's RUF, and the DRC's dozens of armed groups have all been sustained by resource revenues (oil, diamonds, coltan). This is called the *conflict trap*.

4. **Corruption and capture** — large centralized resource rents create extraordinary incentives for elite capture. The political prize becomes control of the resource ministry, not competent governance of the economy broadly.

**Norway vs. Saudi Arabia explained:** Norway discovered oil in 1969 *after* it had already built strong democratic institutions, rule of law, and a broad tax base. The oil went into a sovereign wealth fund (now ~$1.7 trillion) governed by transparent rules, rather than into the political economy of the state. Saudi Arabia had oil before institutions. The oil *became* the institution.

**Investment implication:** Resource-rich developing nations are often *structurally* worse investment environments than their headline resource wealth suggests. The curse creates a specific pattern of risk: boom/bust commodity cycles amplified by political instability, corrupt governance, and Dutch Disease. The investable plays are often *upstream producers* (international oil companies, mining companies) rather than the resource-state itself — and even those require serious political risk analysis.

## IV. The Nuclear Layer — Uranium

No treatment of energy geopolitics is complete without uranium. Nuclear power is the only carbon-free, always-on baseload generation technology currently available at scale. The geopolitics of uranium are underappreciated by most investors.

Key facts:
- Kazakhstan produces ~45% of global uranium (Kazatomprom is the dominant producer)
- Canada (Cameco) and Namibia are the other major sources
- The enrichment supply chain is even more concentrated: Russia's Rosatom controls ~35-40% of global uranium enrichment capacity
- The US was importing ~20% of its uranium from Russia as recently as 2023

The Russia-Ukraine war created immediate questions about the security of this supply chain. Congress passed the Prohibiting Russian Uranium Imports Act in 2024. The result: a structural shift in uranium procurement is underway, with enrichment capacity needing to be rebuilt in the US, EU, UK, Japan, and South Korea.

Meanwhile, the AI boom and the broader electricity demand surge (data centers, EVs) are causing a reappraisal of nuclear power globally. Multiple US tech companies have signed direct power purchase agreements with nuclear operators. The UK, France, and several Eastern European states are expanding nuclear capacity. Japan restarted reactors shut since Fukushima.

**This is a multi-year structural tailwind for uranium prices and uranium producers.**
