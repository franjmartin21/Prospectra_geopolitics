## 1. The Framework: Escalation Ladders

In 1965, military theorist Herman Kahn published *On Escalation: Metaphors and Scenarios*, in which he mapped nuclear conflict dynamics onto a 44-rung ladder — each rung representing a discrete escalation step, from verbal crisis to full nuclear exchange. Kahn's insight was not about nuclear weapons specifically. It was structural: **in any conflict, actors move through predictable stages, and the key to strategy is knowing which rung you are on, what the next rung is, and whether the other side can escalate past it.**

Thomas Schelling (Nobel Prize, 2005) refined this into crisis bargaining theory in *The Strategy of Conflict* (1960): conflicts are not zero-sum wars of annihilation — they are bargaining processes conducted through the controlled application of force and the credible communication of limits. The threat of escalation is often more valuable than escalation itself. The side that can most credibly commit to escalating further has the bargaining leverage.

### The Three Structural Concepts You Need

**1. Escalation dominance:** The ability to raise the cost of conflict at any rung faster than the other side can tolerate. The side with escalation dominance can "own" any level of the ladder it chooses. The US currently holds escalation dominance over Iran militarily — but Iran holds escalation dominance in one specific dimension: maritime denial of the Strait.

**2. The commitment problem:** Both sides in a crisis want the other to believe they will escalate to catastrophic levels if crossed. But rational actors would not actually launch a catastrophic escalation — so the threat is not fully credible. The only way to make a threat credible is to **burn the ships behind you** — take an action that makes backing down costlier than continuing. Iran's seizure of the MSC Francesca was exactly this: not a military necessity, but a commitment device demonstrating that Iran would not stop under current pressure alone.

**3. Focal points:** Schelling's most valuable concept for investors. In any standoff, both sides are looking for a "focal point" — a natural, mutually legible resolution that both sides can accept without losing face. Focal points are often arbitrary (e.g., geographic lines, round numbers, existing treaties) but become real because everyone expects them to be real. In the Hormuz crisis, the focal point is likely a formalized "rules of passage" arrangement — not full resolution, not full war.

## 3. Building the Decision Tree

Kahn's ladder becomes useful for investors when converted into a **decision tree with observable triggers**. The goal: decide in advance what you will do at each fork, so you are not making decisions under stress with incomplete information.

Here is the decision tree for the active long energy position:

```
CURRENT STATE: Long energy (XOM/CVX/LNG) | Brent $105 | Hormuz blocked
Close Rule: 3 consecutive Brent closes below $85 after May 3

BRANCH A: Diplomatic Resolution (Ceasefire holds → Strait reopens)
├── Observable signal: 5+ consecutive days of unimpeded commercial traffic
├── Brent trajectory: $90 → $85 within 4-6 weeks
├── Action: Close rule executes automatically if Brent closes below $85 × 3
└── CEO note: Do NOT close before trigger fires — ceasefire has failed once already

BRANCH B: "Sloppy Peace" (Partial flows, intermittent disruption)
├── Observable signal: 40-60% of normal Hormuz traffic; periodic seizures but no mine detonations
├── Brent trajectory: $95-$105 range
├── Goldman scenario: "maritime trench warfare" — disruption priced in but not worsening
├── Action: HOLD. No change to position or close rule.
└── CEO note: This is our current de facto base case

BRANCH C: Escalation — US Strikes Iranian Military (IRGC or naval assets)
├── Observable signal: Confirmed US military strike on Iranian shore-based assets
├── Brent trajectory: $115-$130 immediate; potentially $150+ (JPMorgan scenario)
├── Action: HOLD. Do not add — this is not predictable and position is already open.
└── CEO note: Consider asymmetric hedge (put on SPY or VIX call) for tail risk to equities

BRANCH D: Catastrophic Escalation (Iranian ballistic strike on Gulf infrastructure)
├── Observable signal: Confirmed strike on Saudi Aramco, UAE, or Kuwait export infrastructure
├── Brent trajectory: $150+ (JPMorgan extreme scenario)
├── Action: HOLD energy long; HEDGE equity exposure; REVIEW gold position (likely positive)
└── CEO note: This rung has low probability but non-zero — the "shoot to destroy" language elevates it

BRANCH E: Accidental De-escalation (Brent collapses on ceasefire news before Strait confirmed open)
├── Observable signal: Market spike down; ceasefire announcement; Brent drops to $90 in a session
├── Action: DO NOT react to announcement — wait for confirmed reopening traffic data
└── CEO note: The April 8 ceasefire is the template. Market moved; reality did not.
```

**The key discipline:** Every branch has an **observable, verifiable trigger**. Not "if the situation seems to be improving" — that is how investors get whipsawed by news cycles. Observable triggers only: Brent price levels, confirmed vessel counts through the Strait, and confirmed military action reports.

## 4. What the OPEC+ May 3 Meeting Actually Means

The agreed 206,000 bbl/day increase for May is structurally irrelevant to current prices and you should understand why.

The fundamental equation of the crisis:
- **Gulf OPEC+ members** (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, Iraq) export through Hormuz.
- Hormuz is blocked.
- Therefore, OPEC+ quota increases from these members **cannot be physically delivered to market** regardless of what the quota says.
- The 206k bbl/day increase is a political signal — it says "we want to produce more" — but it is not supply.

The only OPEC+ decision that materially matters on May 3 is if they announce **production cuts by non-Gulf members** (Russia, Kazakhstan, Algeria) to compensate for the effective Gulf cut — or if they announce **emergency demand-response mechanisms** (drawing down strategic reserves). Watch for those signals, not the headline quota number.

**CEO decision pre-commitment for May 3:**
- Headline quota increase of 200-300k bbl/day: IGNORE. Not decision-relevant.
- Emergency cut of >500k bbl/day by non-Gulf members: Slightly bearish signal (suggests they expect prolonged disruption, but are managing it). Watch but don't close.
- Reserve release coordination (IEA + US SPR): Mildly bearish — reduces immediate supply shock. Still within HOLD range unless Brent moves to $85 close rule.
- No decision / meeting postponed: Neutral to slightly bullish. Confirms they have no solution.

## 5. Historical Grounding: The 1973 and 1990 Comparisons

**1973 Arab Oil Embargo (October War):** OAPEC nations cut production and embargoed the US in retaliation for US support of Israel. Oil quadrupled from $3 to $12/bbl in 6 months. The escalation ladder was clear: Arab defeat (military) → economic retaliation (oil weapon) → US strategic response (Strategic Petroleum Reserve created). Investors who held energy through the embargo and for 2-3 years after made generational returns. Those who sold after the initial spike but before the structural regime change crystallized left most of the move on the table.

**1990 Gulf War (Iraq invades Kuwait):** Oil spiked from $15 to $46/bbl in 4 months as the market priced Hormuz risk. Then — once coalition forces began the air campaign and Iraq's army began collapsing — oil collapsed back to $18 in three weeks. The lesson: in resolved military conflicts, the escalation signal happens fast and the de-escalation is equally fast once the outcome is clear. The 2026 crisis differs because there is no "coalition force with a clear mandate" — this is a naval standoff without an obvious resolution path, which argues for longer duration than 1990.

**The key difference in 2026:** In both 1973 and 1990, there was a structural resolution path visible from the start (diplomatic framework for '73, UN mandate for '90). In 2026, there is no agreed legal framework for Hormuz passage, Iran is fighting for its existential survival (Khamenei was assassinated), and the US has committed to a "shoot to destroy" posture. The conditions for a fast, clean resolution are not present. This argues for treating the current disruption as a **months-long event, not a weeks-long event** — which is the basis for maintaining the HOLD position through May 3.
