## Core Concept: Chips Are the New Oil — But More Concentrated

For most of the 20th century, **crude oil** was the strategic resource that shaped geopolitics. Nations went to war over it. Alliances were built around it. The ability to produce, transport, and deny oil determined the balance of power.

In the 21st century, **advanced semiconductors** are assuming that role — with one critical difference: oil is geographically dispersed across dozens of countries. Advanced chips are made, essentially, in one place.

### What a Semiconductor Is, and Why It Matters

A semiconductor is a chip — an integrated circuit etched onto silicon wafers — that processes information. Modern semiconductors power everything: smartphones, data centers, fighter jets, missile guidance systems, EV batteries, medical imaging equipment, and AI models.

The more advanced the chip (measured in nanometers — smaller is more powerful), the more computationally capable and energy-efficient the device. As of 2026, **cutting-edge chips are manufactured at 3nm and 2nm nodes.** Only one company in the world can do this at scale: **TSMC (Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company).**

This is not a minor market concentration. TSMC produces **over 90% of the world's most advanced chips.** No nation — not the United States, not China, not Germany — has a comparable domestic capability at the frontier.

### The Supply Chain: A Masterclass in Fragility

The semiconductor supply chain is one of the most complex and geographically concentrated in human history:

| Stage | Key Player(s) | Geography |
|---|---|---|
| Chip design (logic) | NVIDIA, AMD, Apple, Qualcomm | USA |
| Chip design (memory) | Samsung, SK Hynix, Micron | South Korea, USA |
| Chip manufacturing | TSMC (leading edge), Samsung | Taiwan, South Korea |
| Lithography machines (EUV) | ASML | Netherlands |
| Advanced packaging | TSMC, OSAT firms | Taiwan, Malaysia |
| Raw silicon wafers | Shin-Etsu, SUMCO | Japan |
| Rare earth inputs | Multiple | China dominates |
| Neon gas (critical for EUV lasers) | Historically Ukraine | Ukraine/US now shifting |

Notice the pattern: every chokepoint is held by a small number of players, many of them in geopolitically exposed locations. ASML is the **only company** capable of producing Extreme Ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machines — the tool that makes advanced chip manufacturing physically possible. Without EUV, you cannot make chips below approximately 7nm. ASML makes roughly 50 of these machines per year. Each costs ~$350 million.

This means the entire frontier of global chip production runs through one Dutch company's factory in Veldhoven.

## Historical Grounding: How We Got Here

**1960s–1980s:** The US invents the integrated circuit (Fairchild, Intel, TI) and leads global production. Chips are a strategic asset but not yet a chokepoint.

**1980s–2000s:** The "fabless" model emerges. American companies (Intel, later Qualcomm, NVIDIA) realize they can outsource manufacturing to specialized foundries in Asia. TSMC is founded in Taiwan in 1987 by Morris Chang, backed in part by the Taiwanese government. The separation of **design** (US dominance) from **fabrication** (Asian specialization) creates the modern supply chain architecture.

**2000s–2010s:** China becomes a massive consumer of chips (for consumer electronics) but cannot produce them. The gap between Chinese chip capability and frontier production widens each generation.

**2018 — The Huawei Shock:** The Trump administration places Huawei on the Entity List, cutting it off from US technology. Because Huawei's chips are designed using US software tools (EDA software from Synopsys and Cadence) and manufactured on equipment with US components, this works. Huawei's smartphone business collapses. China learns it is structurally dependent on US-controlled technology.

**2020 — TSMC Cuts Off Huawei:** Following US pressure, TSMC stops supplying chips to Huawei entirely. China's flagship tech company is decapitated.

**2022 — The CHIPS and Science Act (USA):** The US commits $52 billion to domestic chip manufacturing, with TSMC, Intel, and Samsung receiving subsidies to build US fabs. Simultaneously, the Biden administration issues sweeping export controls targeting advanced chips, chipmaking equipment, and Chinese nationals working at US chip firms.

**2022–2025 — The Export Control Escalation:** The US, Netherlands (ASML), and Japan (Tokyo Electron) form an informal coalition restricting exports of advanced chipmaking equipment to China. China cannot buy the machines needed to catch up.

**2026 — Where We Stand:**
- NVIDIA has reallocated TSMC capacity away from China-targeted H200 chips toward next-gen Vera Rubin hardware, signaling medium-term pessimism on Chinese market access.
- Huawei's Ascend 910C AI accelerator is gaining traction in China, targeting 1.6 million units — China's best domestic alternative to NVIDIA.
- TSMC is expanding US operations (Arizona) under CHIPS Act incentives while facing escalating Taiwan invasion risk assessments (US intelligence: possible 2027).
- The Trump administration is navigating between revenue (some chip sales to China approved) and security (tightening controls on frontier AI chips).
- China's 5-year plan has shifted emphasis from "catch up in chips" to "build around the blockade" — pursuing AI models optimized for less powerful hardware.

## The Taiwan Variable: The Ultimate Tail Risk

Taiwan's strategic importance has a name in geopolitical theory: **the Silicon Shield.** The argument is that Taiwan's role as the indispensable manufacturer of advanced chips gives it a form of deterrence — any military conflict that damages TSMC's fabs destroys an asset China needs as much as the West.

This theory is increasingly contested for two reasons:

1. **China's semiconductor ambitions are long-term:** If China achieves self-sufficiency in chips (even at 5-10 year lag), the Silicon Shield dissolves.
2. **The cost calculus may invert:** A degraded Taiwan (post-conflict) that denies TSMC to the West may be worth more to Beijing than a functioning TSMC it cannot control.

TSMC is actively hedging by building fabs in Arizona, Japan (Kumamoto, Kyushu), and Germany. But frontier production will remain in Taiwan for at least the next 5-7 years. This is a structural risk embedded in every portfolio with technology exposure.

## Investment Implications

### Direct plays on the chip war:

**Bullish:**
- **Semiconductor equipment manufacturers outside China** (ASML, Lam Research, Applied Materials): export control demand is durable; China must keep trying to acquire or replicate.
- **Advanced packaging and HBM memory** (SK Hynix, Micron): AI chip demand drives packaging innovation; the US maintains supply chain integrity.
- **Defense-adjacent chip firms** (companies supplying chips for military electronics, drones, radar): defense spending is secular in a multipolar world.
- **TSMC (TSM)** on a multi-year horizon: the monopoly on advanced manufacturing is durable for 5+ years. Taiwan risk is real but priced partially. Any resolution of Taiwan tensions would be a massive re-rating.
- **US domestic chip capacity plays** (Intel on turnaround optionality, TSMC Arizona indirectly): CHIPS Act spending flows for years.

**Bearish / Cautious:**
- **NVIDIA China revenue**: structurally at risk. Policy uncertainty is permanent. Do not model China as a reliable growth market.
- **Companies with deep China chip supply exposure**: any escalation event hits hard.
- **Generic consumer electronics without pricing power**: the commodity layer of the chain, not the strategic layer.

### Macro / Cross-Asset:
- **Taiwan Dollar and Korean Won**: geopolitical tension events hit both — the EM FX most directly correlated to semiconductor risk.
- **Gold and energy**: chip fab construction is enormously energy-intensive; TSMC's Arizona fab is a major power demand driver. Energy security and chip security are converging.
- **Defense equities**: the AI arms race is accelerating military AI investment. US and European defense contractors are increasingly chip-dependent.

### The Second-Order Play Most Investors Miss:
ASML is arguably the **highest-leverage chokepoint in the entire supply chain.** It has a global monopoly on EUV lithography. There is no substitute. The export control regime has made ASML's position more durable, not less. It is the one company that, if it stopped selling machines, would halt global chip progress entirely within 3-5 years. When you think about where the rent accrues in this system — TSMC gets the headlines; ASML may have the deeper structural moat.

## CEO Portfolio Note

**Structural position:** Long TSMC (TSM) and ASML as core holdings in a geopolitics-aware portfolio. These are not trading positions — they are 3-5 year theses. TSMC at Arizona-hedged valuations is a bet that Taiwan risk is elevated but not imminent and that the monopoly persists. **What to monitor:** ASML quarterly order books (signal on capex confidence); NVIDIA China revenue disclosures; US-China export control escalation/relaxation pace; any reported Chinese EUV progress (the falsifying condition for the entire thesis).

## Reflection Questions

1. **The Silicon Shield dilemma:** If you were Taiwan's government, would you accelerate TSMC's geographic diversification (Arizona, Japan, Germany) to reduce geopolitical risk — or resist it, because the concentration is Taiwan's primary source of deterrence value?
