## 1. The Intellectual Foundation: From Liberal Peace to Weaponized Interdependence

For most of the post-Cold War era, Western policymakers operated on a bedrock assumption: **economic interdependence creates peace**. The logic traces to liberal international relations theory — trade ties raise the cost of conflict, prosperity creates middle classes that demand political stability, and engagement draws rivals into rule-based institutions.

This was the explicit rationale behind China's accession to the World Trade Organization in 2001. The bet was: integrate China into the global economy, and it will converge toward liberal norms.

It was one of the most consequential strategic miscalculations in modern history.

Political scientists Henry Farrell and Abraham Newman introduced the concept of **weaponized interdependence** in 2019 to describe what actually happened: interdependence doesn't just create mutual vulnerability — it creates *asymmetric* vulnerability. Whoever sits at the hub of a network (financial system, supply chain, communications infrastructure) can weaponize that centrality against those at the periphery.

Three structural facts made the US the dominant network hub:
1. **Dollar centrality** — nearly all commodity trade settles in dollars, and most cross-border finance clears through US-connected banks
2. **Technology chokepoints** — US firms or US-allied firms control key nodes: chip design software (EDA tools from Cadence and Synopsys), advanced chip fabrication (TSMC on US-designed equipment), and cloud infrastructure
3. **Legal extraterritoriality** — US law reaches any entity that touches the US financial system or uses US technology, regardless of geography

China's countervailing hub position: it is the world's largest manufacturer, the largest source of rare earth processing (60%+ of global supply), and the dominant assembler in dozens of critical supply chains. The asymmetries run in both directions — but they run differently.

## 2. Historical Grounding: Three Eras of Trade as Statecraft

### Era 1 — The Smoot-Hawley Moment (1930)
The US Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act raised tariffs on 20,000+ imported goods. US trading partners retaliated. Global trade collapsed by ~65% between 1929 and 1934. The lesson embedded into postwar institutions: unilateral tariff escalation is catastrophically self-defeating. This is why GATT (1947) and later the WTO (1995) were built — to constrain beggar-thy-neighbor trade policy.

### Era 2 — Cold War Economic Warfare (1950–1991)
COCOM (Coordinating Committee for Multilateral Export Controls) created a multilateral regime to deny Soviet bloc access to Western technology. This was **export controls as strategy** — not tariffs, but technology denial. The US and its allies maintained clear lines between the security sphere (denied) and the commercial sphere (allowed). This framework held until the USSR collapsed.

### Era 3 — The New Weaponization (2018–present)
The Trump administration's first trade war (2018–2019) marked the return of tariffs as a primary geopolitical instrument, not just a trade remedy. But this era is structurally different from Smoot-Hawley in three ways:

1. **It is targeted, not universal** — tariffs are used against specific countries for specific geopolitical goals (technology transfer, fentanyl, territorial disputes), not general protectionism
2. **It combines multiple instruments** — tariffs, export controls, investment screening (CFIUS), entity lists, and sanctions are deployed simultaneously
3. **Both sides now have sophisticated retaliatory capacity** — China is not a passive target. It has its own export control regime (targeting rare earth magnets, gallium, germanium) and demonstrated willingness to use it

By April 2026, US tariffs on Chinese goods stand at levels not seen since the 1930s — raised by 145 percentage points in the second Trump administration. US imports from China fell by roughly half by mid-2025 before partial stabilization. The US Supreme Court struck down some tariff legal authorities in February 2026, but the bulk of the China tariffs survive under alternative statutory frameworks.

## 3. The Anatomy of a Modern Trade War

A modern trade war has five instruments, each with different escalatory characteristics:

### 3.1 Tariffs
The bluntest instrument. Raises the cost of imported goods, theoretically protecting domestic producers and/or generating fiscal leverage. In practice: costs are largely passed through to domestic consumers and downstream industries. The US auto sector felt this immediately in 2025 when tariffs on imported steel, aluminum, and finished vehicles pushed input costs sharply higher.

**Investment signal:** Tariff escalation → domestic inflation pressure → watch for CPI surprises in affected categories → central bank reaction function becomes unpredictable.

### 3.2 Export Controls
The most surgically powerful instrument in the US toolkit. The Entity List and Export Administration Regulations allow the US to deny any company in the world access to US-origin technology if that company is deemed a national security risk. Huawei's forced exclusion from advanced chips beginning in 2019 showed how devastating this can be.

The logic: if you control the chokepoint (EDA software, advanced chip designs, extreme ultraviolet lithography machines), you can selectively deny entire industries to rivals.

**China's retaliation in kind:** In 2025, China imposed export controls on rare earth permanent magnets and certain semiconductor precursors. Automakers globally — dependent on Chinese rare earth magnets for EV motors — faced immediate supply disruption. This was Beijing's demonstration that it too controls chokepoints.

### 3.3 Investment Screening (CFIUS + Outbound Investment Rules)
The US Committee on Foreign Investment (CFIUS) reviews inbound foreign investment for national security implications. Post-2018, it has been expanded and weaponized — Chinese acquisitions of US tech companies are now effectively blocked.

In 2023, the US added *outbound* investment screening — restricting US capital from flowing into Chinese semiconductor, AI, and quantum computing sectors. This is a structural break: historically, capital was free to flow; now it is subject to national security review.

### 3.4 Sanctions and Dollar Weaponization
The most powerful lever in the system. Dollar dominance means the US can impose secondary sanctions — punishing *third parties* that do business with sanctioned entities, even if those third parties have no US nexus other than using dollar-clearing systems. The 2022 Russia sanctions (freezing $300B in central bank reserves) demonstrated this power at sovereign scale.

But this is a diminishing asset: every aggressive use of dollar weaponization accelerates efforts by adversaries (and nervous neutrals) to develop dollar-alternative systems.

### 3.5 Industrial Policy as Offense
The CHIPS Act (2022), Inflation Reduction Act (2022), and EU equivalents are not just domestic policy — they are competitive displacement strategies. By subsidizing domestic semiconductor fabs, the US aims to move supply chain choke-points onto home soil. This is offensive industrial policy: reshoring the bottlenecks before a rival can weaponize them.

## 4. Supply Chain Topology: Why Geography Matters Again

The 1990s–2010s saw the triumph of *efficiency-optimized* supply chains: just-in-time inventory, single-source suppliers in lowest-cost locations, global fragmentation of production. The implicit assumption: the rules-based trading system is permanent.

COVID-19 (2020–2021) stress-tested that assumption and failed. Semiconductor shortages shut down auto plants. PPE shortages revealed dangerous healthcare supply chain concentration in China. The system optimized for efficiency had no resilience.

The post-2020 paradigm shift: **from efficiency-optimized to resilience-optimized supply chains**. This manifests as:

- **Friend-shoring**: Relocating supply chains to politically aligned countries (Mexico, Vietnam, India, Poland) rather than pure lowest-cost
- **Near-shoring**: Moving production closer to consumption markets, accepting higher costs for shorter, more controllable supply lines
- **Strategic stockpiling**: Government-directed inventory buffers for critical inputs (semiconductors, rare earths, pharmaceuticals)
- **Redundancy by design**: Dual-sourcing, second-supplier qualification, geographic diversification

The McKinsey Global Institute's 2026 analysis shows that geopolitical factors now rank as the #1 risk factor for global logistics leaders — above natural disasters, labor disputes, and financial shocks.

## 5. The Decoupling Question

Is the US and China decoupling? The honest answer: **partial, selective, and incomplete**.

Full decoupling is structurally implausible in the medium term. China is embedded in too many global supply chains as both supplier and consumer. US companies cannot simply exit — Apple manufactures in China, Tesla sells there, Walmart sources there.

What is happening is **bifurcation at the technological frontier**: in semiconductors, AI, and dual-use military technology, hard decoupling is underway and accelerating. In consumer goods, commodities, and lower-tech manufacturing, integration persists — even if routed through third countries (the "Vietnam as China bypass" phenomenon, which US tariff policy has begun to address).

The investment implication: do not model this as binary (coupled / decoupled). Model it as sector-by-sector with a geopolitical premium on strategic industries.

## 6. Investment Implications

### Equities — Sector Effects
- **Defense & industrial policy beneficiaries**: Arms manufacturers, domestic chip fabricators (Intel, Micron post-CHIPS Act), reshoring plays in industrials
- **Supply chain complexity plays**: Logistics companies, supply chain software (resilience monitoring, multi-source inventory management), nearshore manufacturing
- **Vulnerable sectors**: Any business with concentrated China supply chains and no clear alternative (consumer electronics assembly, rare earth-dependent EV makers)
- **Emerging market rotation**: Vietnam, India, Mexico, Indonesia — the primary beneficiaries of supply chain diversification. Watch these as structural longs on a 3–5 year horizon, not tactical trades

### Commodities
- **Rare earths and critical minerals**: China controls ~85% of rare earth processing. Export controls on these are China's most credible retaliatory lever. This creates a structural bull case for ex-China rare earth producers (MP Materials in the US, Lynas in Australia)
- **Industrial metals (copper, aluminum)**: Tariffs on aluminum and steel create domestic pricing premiums; watch the spread between LME and US domestic prices
- **Energy**: Tariff-driven deglobalization is energy-inflationary — longer supply chains, more redundancy, and more industrial policy all require more energy

### Currencies
- **Tariff shocks → dollar strength (short-term)**: Risk-off flows into dollars; foreign exporters to the US see earnings compress
- **Dollar weaponization → dollar weakness (structural)**: Each aggressive use of sanctions and trade restrictions incentivizes dollar alternatives. The long-run trajectory of dollar dominance is directionally lower — the timeline is uncertain (decades, not years)
- **EM FX under pressure**: Countries caught between US-China trade blocs face currency instability — especially those with high US tariff exposure or China supply chain dependence

### Fixed Income
- **Tariff inflation → steeper yield curve**: Persistent tariff-driven inflation argues for long-end yields to remain elevated; duration risk is real
- **Sovereign spreads**: Countries with clear geopolitical alignment (US bloc vs. China bloc) see diverging borrowing costs; geopolitical alignment is becoming a credit factor

## 7. The Signal vs. Noise Distinction

In trade war coverage, 90% of daily news is noise. The signals to watch:

| Signal | Noise |
|---|---|
| Changes to Entity List and export control scope | Day-to-day tariff rhetoric |
| CFIUS / outbound investment rule expansions | Bilateral summit communiqués |
| WTO dispute filings (reveal escalation intent) | Presidential tweets about trade deficits |
| Supply chain survey data (ISM, PMI supplier delivery times) | Individual company tariff exemption requests |
| China's rare earth export quota announcements | Short-term trade balance data |
| Friend-shoring investment flows (FDI into Vietnam, India, Mexico) | Monthly bilateral trade figures |

The CEO's rule: **monitor structural changes to the rules, not the level of the tariff in any given week**.

## 8. Databricks Angle

This lesson maps directly to your Phase 1 pipeline priorities.

**Dataset: GDELT Global Knowledge Graph (GKG)**
GDELT tracks global news events and tags them by theme, location, and actor. Trade war events — tariff announcements, export control notices, supply chain disruption reports — are tagged in real time. A pipeline that filters GDELT for trade-related themes (`ECON_TRADE`, `SANCTION`, `EXPORT_CONTROL`) and cross-references with market data can measure whether trade policy announcements predict commodity or currency moves.

**Pipeline idea: Trade Policy Shock Detector**
- Ingest: GDELT GKG daily file (available free via Google BigQuery public dataset `gdelt-bq.gdeltv2.gkg`)
- Filter: Themes containing `TRADE`, `TARIFF`, `SANCTION`, `EXPORT_CONTROL`
- Join: Against Yahoo Finance daily closes for copper, rare earth ETFs (REMX), Vietnam ETF (VNM), USD/CNY, USD/VND
- Lag analysis: Do spikes in trade-related GDELT coverage precede asset moves at 1-day, 3-day, 7-day lags?

This is a concrete, buildable pipeline that tests the core investment thesis. The signal may or may not exist — but finding out is the point.

**Secondary dataset: FRED (Federal Reserve Economic Data)**
- US trade balance by country (monthly)
- Producer price indices for tariff-affected sectors (steel, aluminum, semiconductors)
- Import price indices

These validate whether tariff events show up in macro data and with what lag.
