## 1. Core Concept: Energy as Geopolitical Infrastructure

Energy is not a commodity like any other. Unlike copper or wheat, the global energy system is a **physical, financial, and political infrastructure** that the world's entire industrial base depends on. Whoever controls critical nodes of that infrastructure — production choke points, transit routes, pricing mechanisms — holds structural power over every economy connected to it.

There are four layers to understand:

### Layer 1: Physical Chokepoints
The world's oil and gas must move through a small number of geographic bottlenecks. These are:

| Chokepoint | Daily Flow | What Passes Through |
|---|---|---|
| Strait of Hormuz | ~21 million barrels/day | Saudi, UAE, Kuwait, Iraqi, Iranian oil |
| Strait of Malacca | ~16 million barrels/day | Persian Gulf oil to China, Japan, South Korea |
| Suez Canal / SUMED Pipeline | ~9 million barrels/day | Gulf oil to Europe |
| Turkish Straits (Bosphorus) | ~3 million barrels/day | Russian Caspian and Black Sea oil |
| Bab-el-Mandeb | ~6 million barrels/day | Gulf to Europe via Red Sea |

**The strategic implication:** any power that can threaten, close, or control these chokepoints holds the world's industrial economies hostage. This is why Iran's perpetual threat to mine the Strait of Hormuz is a serious deterrent — not because Iran could win a war with the US, but because even a 10-day closure would trigger a global recession.

### Layer 2: Producer Concentration (OPEC+ Architecture)
Roughly 40% of the world's traded oil flows through OPEC+ decisions. Saudi Arabia — as the world's largest exporter and the only major producer with significant *swing capacity* — effectively functions as the central bank of the oil market. When Riyadh chooses to add or remove 1 million barrels/day from supply, it moves the global price.

**The OPEC+ coalition (2016–present)** married Saudi Arabia with Russia — two powers with often competing geopolitical interests — under the shared incentive of higher oil prices. This arrangement has repeatedly fractured under geopolitical stress (notably March 2020) and repeatedly reconstituted under fiscal pressure. Understanding its internal tensions is essential for commodity positioning.

Key fault lines inside OPEC+:
- **Saudi vs. UAE:** UAE has expanded production capacity and increasingly defects from quotas
- **Saudi vs. Russia:** Russia needs oil revenue for the war in Ukraine; Riyadh needs it for Vision 2030 and to maintain dollar peg credibility
- **Core OPEC vs. marginal members:** Nigeria, Angola, and others chronically overproduce because their fiscal breakevens require it

### Layer 3: Natural Gas — The Balkanized Market
Unlike oil, natural gas cannot easily be arbitraged across the globe. Until liquefied natural gas (LNG) infrastructure reaches scale, natural gas markets are **regional and pipeline-dependent** — which makes them tools of political leverage in ways oil markets are not.

Russia's strategic exploitation of this fact — supplying 40% of European gas via Nordstream 1, Yamal, and TurkStream — was the centerpiece of its European leverage for 15 years. Gazprom was as much a foreign policy instrument as an energy company.

The 2022 invasion of Ukraine and subsequent destruction of Nordstream permanently restructured the European gas market:
- European LNG import terminals expanded at emergency speed
- Norway surpassed Russia as Europe's top gas supplier within 12 months
- The US LNG export complex (Sabine Pass, Freeport, Corpus Christi) became strategically critical infrastructure
- TTF (Dutch Title Transfer Facility) gas prices became a European economic stability indicator

### Layer 4: Uranium — The Quiet Nuclear Equation
Nuclear power is the only zero-carbon baseload power source available at industrial scale. After a decade of retreat following Fukushima (2011), the nuclear renaissance is underway: France is recommitting to its fleet, the US is restarting mothballed plants, and China is building approximately 20 reactors simultaneously.

**The geopolitical vulnerability:** The world's uranium supply chain is dangerously concentrated.

| Step | Concentration |
|---|---|
| Uranium mining | Kazakhstan produces ~45% of world supply |
| Conversion (UF6) | Russia's Rosatom controls ~27% of global capacity |
| Enrichment | Russia controls ~44% of global enrichment capacity |

Western nuclear power plants — including US reactors — have been importing Russian enriched uranium for decades. This dependency is structurally similar to Europe's gas dependency, with a 5–10 year timeline to diversify (enrichment capacity cannot be built quickly).

## 2. The Resource Curse — When Wealth Becomes a Trap

The resource curse (also called the *paradox of plenty*) is one of the most robust findings in development economics: countries with large natural resource endowments often grow more slowly, develop weaker institutions, and suffer more political instability than resource-poor countries.

**Why does this happen?**

1. **Dutch Disease:** Resource exports strengthen the currency, making all other exports uncompetitive. The non-resource economy atrophies. When the resource boom ends, there is nothing else.

2. **Revenue without taxation:** Governments that fund themselves through resource royalties don't need to tax citizens — and therefore citizens have less leverage to demand accountability. "No taxation without representation" works in reverse: no taxation, no representation demanded.

3. **Elite capture:** Resource rents concentrate in the hands of whoever controls extraction — creating oligarchies with every incentive to capture the state and block institutional development.

4. **Boom-bust cycles:** Resource prices are volatile. States that budget around boom prices are repeatedly devastated by busts.

**Case studies:**
- **Nigeria:** Africa's largest oil producer, yet 40%+ of the population lives below the poverty line. Boko Haram and Niger Delta militancy are partly downstream of resource mismanagement.
- **Venezuela:** The world's largest proven oil reserves; collapsed into hyperinflation and mass emigration under Chavismo's resource-dependent model.
- **Norway counterexample:** Established the Government Pension Fund Global (sovereign wealth fund) in 1990, sequestering oil revenues and investing them in global equities. Now the world's largest SWF at $1.7 trillion. The institutional design — not the oil — determined the outcome.

**Investment implication:** Resource-rich EM sovereigns are not automatically attractive. The critical analytical question is: does the country have the institutional capacity to avoid the resource curse? Norway did. Nigeria, Venezuela, and Angola did not.

## 3. Concrete Example: The 2022 European Energy Crisis

**What happened:**
Following Russia's February 2022 invasion of Ukraine, Western Europe faced the most severe energy shock since the 1970s oil embargo. Russia progressively throttled and ultimately cut gas flows to Europe through Nordstream 1 (citing maintenance, later confirmed sabotage). TTF gas prices peaked at approximately €350/MWh in August 2022 — versus a pre-crisis norm of €15–25/MWh. European industrial electricity prices followed. German industrial output contracted. European governments spent approximately €800 billion on energy subsidies and price caps to prevent economic collapse.

**The geopolitical mechanics:**
Russia was using Gazprom as an instrument of coercive diplomacy — attempting to fracture European NATO support for Ukraine by threatening a cold, dark winter. The strategy ultimately failed: Europe found alternative supply faster than Moscow expected (via Norwegian, Algerian, Qatari LNG, and emergency US LNG exports), and the political will to support Ukraine held.

**Why it failed:**
1. The threat was held too long — Europe had 12 months to build alternative infrastructure before Russian flows fully stopped
2. LNG spot markets, while tight, provided enough marginal supply to prevent catastrophe
3. European demand destruction (industrial shutdowns, efficiency measures) was faster than Russia anticipated
4. The US LNG export complex ramped immediately

**The structural outcome (now permanent):**
- Russian pipeline gas to Europe is effectively finished as a strategic dependency
- European LNG import capacity grew from ~170 bcm to ~300+ bcm annually
- The US becomes Europe's dominant gas supplier — cementing a transatlantic energy relationship
- Germany's energy policy is permanently restructured (though still debating nuclear restart pace)

**The investment thesis at each stage:**
- *Pre-invasion long:* European gas utilities with LNG exposure, US LNG exporters (CHENIERE, VENTURE GLOBAL)
- *Peak crisis:* Short European energy-intensive industrial equities (BASF, Covestro)
- *Recovery phase:* Long European energy infrastructure buildout (Snam, Enagas, grid buildout plays)
- *Long-term structural:* Long uranium (nuclear renaissance), long US LNG exporters, short Russian energy assets (effectively stranded)

| Resource curse + political instability in EM | Sovereign EM bonds, EM equity | Short / underweight |
| LNG infrastructure buildout | Gas utilities, pipeline companies | Long (multi-year) |

### Current CEO Views (April 2026)
- **Uranium:** Long thesis intact. The enrichment dependency on Russia is slowly being resolved (Centrus Energy in US, Orano in France, Urenco expanding) but 3–5 year timeline. Uranium spot remains structurally undersupplied as new reactor demand outpaces mine production restarts.
- **US LNG exporters:** Structural long. Europe's shift from Russian pipeline to global LNG is irreversible. Cheniere Energy (LNG) and the emerging second wave (Venture Global) are infrastructure plays, not commodity plays — they operate on long-term offtake contracts.
- **OPEC+:** Monitoring cohesion carefully. UAE quota defection risk is rising. If the coalition fractures in 2026, WTI could see significant downside (sub-$60 scenario). This argues for hedged oil exposure, not outright long.
- **Nigerian/Venezuelan sovereign bonds:** Avoid. Classic resource curse dynamics with institutional deficits.
