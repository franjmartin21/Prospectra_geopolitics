## Opening Framing Question

Here is the question that should sit with you through this entire lesson:

> **Why does the United States get to run permanent trade deficits, borrow at lower rates than any other nation, and impose financial sanctions that cripple entire economies — simply because of which currency it issues?**

Most people answer this with "because the dollar is the reserve currency." That answer is correct and completely unsatisfying. *Why* is it the reserve currency? What holds that system together? What happens to US power — and global markets — if it starts to unravel?

Those are the real questions. Let's build the framework.

---

## 1. How the Dollar System Was Born: Bretton Woods (1944)

In July 1944, with World War II still ongoing but the Allied victory increasingly certain, 730 delegates from 44 nations gathered at a resort in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire. Their task: design the post-war monetary order before the war ended, so economic chaos wouldn't follow military victory the way it had after 1918.

The agreement they reached had three pillars:

1. **Gold-dollar anchor:** The US dollar would be fixed to gold at $35/oz. Every other currency would be fixed to the dollar. The US held roughly 2/3 of global gold reserves at the time — this wasn't arbitrary.

2. **New institutions:** The IMF (to manage balance-of-payments crises) and the World Bank (to finance reconstruction) were created. Both headquartered in Washington. Both structured to give the US dominant voting power.

3. **Capital controls permitted:** Countries could control cross-border capital flows to protect their fixed exchange rates. Free trade in goods was the goal; free movement of capital was not yet on the table.

The Bretton Woods system gave the world monetary stability for roughly 25 years. Trade expanded. Western Europe and Japan rebuilt. The dollar became the universal medium of international commerce.

**But it contained a fatal flaw — the Triffin Dilemma.**

Robert Triffin, a Belgian-American economist, identified it in 1960: for the dollar to function as the world's reserve currency, the US had to supply enough dollars to meet global demand — which meant running persistent current account deficits. But running persistent deficits would eventually undermine confidence in the dollar's gold convertibility. The system required the US to simultaneously be the world's banker *and* a deficit nation. Those two roles are in permanent tension.

---

## 2. The Nixon Shock (1971): The Tether Breaks

By the late 1960s, the US was running large deficits to finance both the Vietnam War and the Great Society domestic programs. Foreign central banks — especially France under de Gaulle, who was openly contemptuous of American monetary privilege — began converting their dollar reserves into gold. The gold window was under pressure.

On August 15, 1971, President Nixon announced the US would no longer convert dollars into gold. No advance notice to allies. No multilateral negotiation. The gold standard ended in a Sunday evening television address.

The world had a choice: find a new anchor, or accept a dollar standard backed by nothing but US credibility and market inertia.

They chose the dollar — because what was the alternative?

This is the key insight: **the dollar didn't win by design after 1971. It won by default.** No other currency had the depth of markets, the liquidity, or the institutional infrastructure to replace it.

---

## 3. The Petrodollar System: The New Anchor

Nixon's Treasury Secretary John Connally famously told foreign finance ministers: *"The dollar is our currency, but it's your problem."*

What stabilized the dollar after 1971 was a deal that rarely gets discussed in economics textbooks: the petrodollar arrangement with Saudi Arabia (1974).

The deal, negotiated by Henry Kissinger and Treasury Secretary William Simon:
- Saudi Arabia agrees to price all oil sales in US dollars exclusively
- OPEC countries recycle their dollar surpluses into US Treasury bonds ("petrodollar recycling")
- The US provides Saudi Arabia military protection and security guarantees

The effect was immediate and structural: every country in the world that needed oil — which was every country — needed dollars. The demand for dollars was no longer backed by gold. It was backed by energy.

This is why the dollar's reserve status and US energy policy are inseparable. It's also why any disruption to dollar-denominated oil pricing is treated as a national security issue in Washington.

---

## 4. The "Exorbitant Privilege" — What Dollar Dominance Actually Buys

French Finance Minister Valéry Giscard d'Estaing coined the phrase "exorbitant privilege" in the 1960s to describe the US advantage. Let's be precise about what it consists of:

**a) Borrowing at a structural discount**
When the world needs dollars to settle trade and hold reserves, there is permanent demand for US Treasuries. This demand suppresses US borrowing costs below what they would otherwise be. Estimates range from 50–100 basis points of structural yield advantage. On $30+ trillion in federal debt, that is an enormous annual subsidy.

**b) Seigniorage at scale**
The US prints dollars. The world holds them. Holding dollars is effectively lending to the US at zero interest. The US gets real goods and services; the world gets paper (or digital) claims.

**c) Sanctions as a weapon of state**
The dollar's role in SWIFT, correspondent banking, and commodity pricing means the US can cut any country off from the global financial system by denying access to dollar clearing. Iran, Russia, Venezuela — dollar sanctions are arguably the most powerful non-military tool of statecraft ever developed. This tool only exists because of reserve currency status.

**d) Structural current account deficit tolerance**
Other countries must earn the foreign exchange to service their debts. The US simply issues more of the currency everyone else needs. This is why the US can run persistent trade deficits without a balance-of-payments crisis — a luxury no other nation has.

## 5. Is Dollar Dominance Eroding? The 2026 Landscape

Here is where the lesson connects to the present moment — and where the investment implications get sharp.

**The data:**
- Dollar share of global FX reserves: ~57% (down from 72% in 2001)
- Dollar's share of global forex trading: ~88% (remarkably stable)
- Oil trades in non-dollar currencies: ~20% of transactions (up from near-zero in 2019)
- BRICS Pay platform launched in 2025-26: reportedly reduced USD usage in intra-bloc trade by ~two-thirds
- EM central bank gold purchases: at multi-decade highs since 2022 (Russia sanctions triggered this)

**What accelerated the shift:**
The February 2022 decision to freeze $300 billion of Russia's sovereign reserves held in Western financial institutions was a watershed moment. Every non-Western central bank drew the same conclusion: holding reserves in dollars is not just an economic decision — it is a geopolitical bet on permanent alignment with the US. Countries that are neutral, or adversarial, began diversifying into gold and bilateral currency arrangements immediately.

**What is NOT happening:**
A sudden dollar collapse. The dollar retains structural advantages that cannot be replicated in years:
- Deepest, most liquid bond market in the world (US Treasuries)
- Rule of law protecting property rights for foreign investors
- No credible single alternative: the euro has political fragmentation risk; the yuan has capital controls; gold can't scale; "The Unit" BRICS digital currency remains conceptual

**The honest assessment:** The dollar is losing market share at the margin, not losing dominance. The transition, if it happens, will be measured in decades, not years. But the *direction* is clear, and the *pace* is accelerating. The Russia sanctions were a catalyst that cannot be un-rung.

## 6. Investment Implications

This framework has direct, actionable implications for portfolio construction:

**GOLD — Structurally Bullish**
EM central banks are diversifying *into* gold as a sanction-proof reserve asset. This is not a cyclical trade — it is a structural reallocation that will continue regardless of interest rate cycles. Central bank gold demand is a long-duration structural bid. *Position: long gold as a geopolitical hedge, 6–18 month horizon and beyond.*

**USD — Trend Weakening, Not Collapse**
A slow erosion of reserve share means modest, secular dollar weakness over years. This is a tail wind for dollar-denominated commodity prices (priced in USD, so dollar weakness = commodity price support) and for EM assets (which benefit when dollar funding costs ease). Not a crash trade — a positioning tilt.

**US Treasuries — Diminished Structural Buyer Base**
If foreign central banks reduce dollar reserve accumulation, the structural bid for US Treasuries decreases. Combined with US fiscal deficits, this points to upward pressure on long-term yields over time. *Watch for term premium expansion.*

**EM FX Diversification — Selective Opportunity**
Countries with current account surpluses, commodity exports, and non-aligned geopolitical positioning (Brazil, Indonesia, Gulf states, India to a degree) have structural reasons to strengthen their currencies relative to USD over a long horizon. Specific bilateral currency arrangements (China-Brazil, Russia-India) reduce dollar demand in specific trade corridors.

**Commodities — Structural Long**
The petrodollar's partial erosion, combined with EM central bank gold buying and critical mineral demand (covered in Lesson 5), supports a secular commodity bull thesis. Oil priced partially in yuan, copper priced in renminbi, gold as reserve — all point in the same direction.
