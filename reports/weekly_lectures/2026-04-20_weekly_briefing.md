## Event 1: Hormuz Ceasefire Holds — Oil Collapses 28% from Peak

**What happened:**
The US-Iran ceasefire announced April 8 has held for nine sessions as of April 19. Iran confirmed the Strait of Hormuz is "completely open." Brent crude, which peaked at almost $128/barrel on April 2, has fallen to ~$87/barrel as of April 17 — a 28% decline from peak in under three weeks. The ceasefire technically expires April 21. A second diplomatic round in Islamabad is under discussion but not yet scheduled. The US blockade — involving 10,000+ troops and over a dozen Navy ships — remains active in the Gulf of Oman and Arabian Sea. The IEA estimates oil flows will not fully normalize until July 2026, even with the strait fully open. OPEC+ meets May 3.

**Geopolitical significance:**
This is the most important single development of the past two weeks for portfolio positioning. The partial resolution demonstrates that the US has a workable off-ramp from the Iran conflict — but has not taken it fully. The ceasefire is fragile: it was not mediated by a durable multilateral framework, the underlying Iranian succession crisis is unresolved, and both sides retain military positioning for escalation. The IEA's July normalization timeline reflects real infrastructure damage to Iran's maritime coordination capacity. This is not peace — it is a suspension.

The geopolitical architecture is more fragile than the oil price drop suggests. Iran remains fractured internally; whoever controls the state after Khamenei's killing has every incentive to reassert leverage over energy chokepoints as a negotiating chip. The US has demonstrated willingness to strike Iranian infrastructure, which compresses Iran's conventional deterrent but increases its irregular and proxy options.

**Investment implications:**
- **Brent crude (~$87):** Near the "wrong if" threshold for the Long Energy call ($85). Do not close the call — but the risk premium compression is real and the thesis requires updating. See CEO Portfolio Note below.
- **LNG infrastructure:** Partially decoupled from spot price; long-term contract values remain elevated. Still constructive.
- **Energy equities:** XOM, CVX YTD +40% peak, now giving back gains. The structural thesis (Hormuz risk, OPEC+ discipline, $85+ floor) remains intact if ceasefire holds. Watch the May 3 OPEC+ meeting for supply discipline signal.
- **European industrial recovery:** Lower energy input costs reduce the stagflation squeeze partially. Do not flip the European industrials short yet — the ceasefire may not hold and the recession data is real.

## Event 2: IMF World Economic Outlook — "Global Economy in the Shadow of War"

**What happened:**
The IMF released its April 2026 World Economic Outlook on April 14, cutting global growth to 3.1% for 2026 (down from a pre-conflict projection of 3.4%). The title — "Global Economy in the Shadow of War" — is not subtle. The IMF presented three scenarios:

| Scenario | 2026 GDP Growth | 2026 Inflation |
|---|---|---|
| Base (limited conflict) | 3.1% | Modestly rising |
| Adverse (prolonged energy shock) | 2.5% | 5.4% |
| Severe (energy disruption into 2027) | 2.0% | >6.0% |

**Geopolitical significance:**
The IMF downgrade is institutional confirmation of the thesis we have been building since April 4. The stagflation risk — tariff-driven inflation layered onto an energy shock — is now consensus. The severe scenario (2% global growth) would represent the worst peacetime economic performance since the Global Financial Crisis. The "shadow of war" framing signals that the IMF now treats geopolitical risk as the primary macroeconomic variable — not monetary policy, not AI productivity, not China cyclicals. That is a structural shift in how the world's principal economic forecaster communicates risk.

**Investment implications:**
- **Gold:** IMF adverse/severe scenarios are gold's best friend — stagflation with central banks caught between inflation and recession. The Long Gold call is reinforced.
- **TIPS (inflation-linked bonds):** Adverse scenario materializing → real rates compressed, TIPS outperform nominal Treasuries.
- **EM sovereign debt:** Adverse scenario → EM risk-off, sovereign spreads widen. Be selective — differentiate commodity exporters (Brazil, Indonesia) from import-dependent EMs.
- **Global equities generally:** 2.5–3.1% global growth is below prepandemic averages and insufficient to sustain current equity multiples in rate-sensitive sectors. Value tilt over growth in this environment.

## Event 3: Trump Threatens 50% Tariffs on China — Pre-Summit Escalation

**What happened:**
On April 13, Trump threatened to impose 50% tariffs on China following reports that Beijing was preparing an arms shipment to Iran. China's current effective US tariff rate is ~31.6%, already the highest of any country. The tariff threat comes weeks before a scheduled US-China May summit — Trump's first visit to China in eight years — creating a deliberately destabilizing pre-negotiation dynamic. Separately, the USTR launched two new Section 301 investigations into Chinese excess capacity and forced labor; public hearings scheduled for May 5. The Supreme Court's early 2026 ruling that IEEPA-based tariffs are unlawful has forced the administration to route tariffs through Section 301 and Section 232 — slower but durable.

**Geopolitical significance:**
The arms-to-Iran report, if accurate, represents a direct Chinese intervention in the US's most active hot war. Beijing providing weapons to Tehran would constitute a de facto proxy war by the world's second-largest economy against the United States' stated security interests. This is the most dangerous escalation scenario in the US-China relationship since the Taiwan Strait crises of the 1990s.

Three possible interpretations: (1) The report is accurate and China is deliberately testing US tolerance during the summit preparation; (2) The report is accurate and reflects internal Chinese military/strategic faction behavior that Xi may not fully control; (3) The report is a US strategic leak designed to give Trump leverage in May summit negotiations. All three are plausible. None is benign.

**Investment implications:**
- **China-exposed supply chains (bearish, watch):** Any confirmation of arms delivery to Iran triggers a new escalation spiral. 50% tariffs on top of 31.6% would be economically catastrophic for remaining US-China trade. Reduce exposure to companies with significant China sourcing ahead of the May summit.
- **Taiwan/semiconductor (elevated risk):** If US-China hostility deepens over Iran proxy dynamics, Taiwan Strait risk premium increases. Long-duration semiconductor stocks face a vol event.
- **Brazilian agribusiness:** Structural thesis strengthened. China decoupling from US agriculture accelerates under any escalation scenario. Brazil thesis is more robust than it was two weeks ago.
- **Defense:** Any Iran-China axis story accelerates US and allied defense procurement. European defense call strengthened.

## Event 4: Ukraine-Russia Easter Ceasefire — 32 Hours, Widely Violated

**What happened:**
Putin announced a 32-hour Orthodox Easter ceasefire on April 10; Zelensky confirmed Ukraine would honor it. The pause ran from 4pm Moscow time Saturday through midnight Sunday. Both sides accused each other of multiple violations within hours. The UAE brokered a prisoner-of-war exchange — 175 soldiers per side — mediated alongside the ceasefire. Peace talks remain formally stalled; Russia rejected Ukraine's proposal to freeze the conflict along current front lines, demanding instead full Ukrainian withdrawal from Russian-claimed Donetsk territory. Washington's diplomatic bandwidth remains consumed by the Iran situation.

**Geopolitical significance:**
The ceasefire itself is noise. The POW exchange is a signal — it required functioning back-channel diplomacy between Moscow and Kyiv, mediated by a Gulf state with relations to both sides. The UAE's role is notable: a US partner that has refused to sanction Russia, maintains energy-driven pragmatism with Moscow, and is now inserting itself as the Middle East's premier diplomatic broker. The real significance is structural: US diplomatic distraction from Ukraine is now a persistent condition, not a temporary one. Europe — France, UK, Poland — is managing the Ukraine file without American leadership. That process is slow, imperfect, and under-resourced.

**Investment implications:**
- **European defense:** Nothing changes. Structural rearmament continues regardless of ceasefire status. Call remains open.
- **Natural gas (watch):** A genuine ceasefire framework, when it eventually comes, could depress European gas prices as Russian pipeline politics become negotiable again. Not a near-term risk but a 12-month scenario to watch.
- **Eastern European equities:** Polish and Baltic markets remain supported by NATO spending commitments. No change.
- **Ukrainian reconstruction:** Still speculative. The POW exchange and back-channel activity suggest the long diplomatic road is beginning. Start the research clock — this will be a multi-decade reconstruction opportunity when it arrives.

## Event 5: Dollar Weakens for Third Consecutive Week — EUR/USD at 1.17

**What happened:**
The US Dollar Index (DXY) stabilized around 98–100 but remained on track for its third consecutive weekly decline as of April 18. EUR/USD is trading at ~1.17. Analysts cite improving prospects for US-Iran de-escalation reducing safe-haven demand, but structural drivers — US tariff-driven stagflation, political pressure on Fed independence, dollar's eroding credibility premium — remain firmly in place. Full-year 2026 consensus sees DXY in the low 90s if the Fed begins cutting rates in H2. Under the IMF's adverse scenario, dollar dynamics are unpredictable: stagflation with geopolitical risk could maintain some safe-haven premium even as dollar purchasing power erodes.

**Geopolitical significance:**
The dollar's reserve currency status is a long-running story, not a weekly event — but each week it weakens is a data point confirming the directional trend. Three simultaneous forces are compressing the dollar's credibility premium: (1) US trade policy unpredictability, (2) political pressure on Fed independence, (3) US active military engagement creating fiscal and strategic overextension. None of these resolves in 90 days. The euro and CNY are pressing their case into the gap.

**Investment implications:**
- **Gold:** Remains the core beneficiary of dollar structural weakness + geopolitical uncertainty. Call unchanged.
- **EUR/USD:** At 1.17, tracking toward analyst consensus of 1.20. USD-based investors holding European assets are benefiting from FX translation. European defense ETFs denominated in EUR are up in USD terms beyond their local-currency returns.
- **EM currencies (selective):** BRL, INR benefit from dollar weakness + commodity tailwinds. BRL specifically benefits from the Brazil agricultural thesis. Indonesian rupiah and Malaysian ringgit benefit from commodity exposure (palm oil, LNG, coal). Differentiate from dollar-indebted EM sovereigns where dollar weakness provides less relief.
- **US multinationals:** FX translation headwind on foreign earnings reported in USD. Underweight remains appropriate.
