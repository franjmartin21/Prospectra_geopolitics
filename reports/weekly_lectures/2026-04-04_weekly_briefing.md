## Event 1: US-Iran War & Strait of Hormuz Closure

**What happened:**
On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched coordinated airstrikes against Iran, killing Supreme Leader Khamenei. Iran responded with missile and drone strikes on US bases and regional allies, and on March 4 declared the Strait of Hormuz closed, attacking ships attempting to transit. As of April 4, Trump has extended a deadline to April 6 for Iran to reopen the Strait, threatening strikes on Iranian power infrastructure. A virtual multi-country summit on freedom of navigation is underway.

**Geopolitical significance:**
This is the largest disruption to global energy supply since the 1970s oil embargo — and potentially the most consequential single event in energy geopolitics of the 21st century. The Strait of Hormuz carries approximately 20% of global oil and LNG flows. The closure has fractured the already-fragile Middle East security architecture and drawn in every major power simultaneously. The US is exposed to a two-front strategic overextension — tariff war with China while fighting a hot war in the Persian Gulf. This weakens US leverage in both theaters.

**Investment implications:**
- **Oil/Energy (strongly bullish):** Brent surged to $126/barrel at peak; currently ~$109–111. Energy sector YTD +40%. With the April 6 deadline unresolved, further supply disruption risk is real. Long energy exposure remains the highest-conviction commodity trade in the market right now.
- **LNG (bullish):** Europe's dependency on LNG alternatives to Russian gas makes it doubly exposed. European LNG infrastructure plays and US LNG exporters benefit.
- **European equities (bearish):** ECB warning of stagflation. Germany and Italy at technical recession risk by end of 2026. Short or underweight European industrials and auto sectors.
- **Defense equities (strongly bullish):** NATO allies accelerating rearmament. Defense budgets expanding across Europe and Asia. Multi-year structural tailwind.
- **Shipping/tankers (uncertain):** Route diversification around Hormuz creates both risk (war premium) and opportunity (alternative tanker routes, Cape of Good Hope traffic surge).

## Event 2: US Tariff Regime — Peak Consumer Impact Window Opens

**What happened:**
The "Liberation Day" tariff announcement of April 2, 2025 is now one year old. The average effective US tariff rate stands at ~10% (down from peaks during escalation phases). Economists have consistently modeled a 12–18 month lag before full consumer price impact materializes — placing peak pressure squarely in the April–October 2026 window. Separately, Trump announced intent to raise rates to 15% in February 2026.

**Geopolitical significance:**
The tariff regime represents the most significant US trade policy shift since Smoot-Hawley. It has accelerated de-globalization, accelerated friend-shoring and supply chain reorganization, and weakened the rules-based multilateral trade order. The WTO is functionally sidelined. Average US household is absorbing approximately $1,500 in annual added costs. The political economy of this becomes dangerous if inflation re-accelerates into midterms.

**Investment implications:**
- **US consumer staples/retail (bearish):** Peak pass-through period arriving. Margin compression for importers; consumer wallets squeezed.
- **US domestic manufacturing/reshoring plays (bullish):** Policy-driven capex in onshore production. Industrial REITs, domestic steel, semiconductor fabs.
- **US inflation expectations (bearish for bonds):** Tariff-driven inflation + Iran oil shock = stagflation scenario. Short duration US Treasuries, inflation-linked bonds (TIPS) attractive.
- **EM exporters hurt by US tariffs (bearish/uncertain):** Mexico, Vietnam, Bangladesh face ongoing exposure. Watch for trade diversion beneficiaries — India, Indonesia.

## Event 3: China–US Trade War — Structural Break in Agricultural Trade

**What happened:**
US soybean exports to China fell 78% through mid-2025; corn exports collapsed 99%. China has essentially stopped buying US agricultural commodities and has redirected sourcing to Brazil, Argentina, and Australia. In March 2026, China launched two new WTO-format investigations into US trade practices targeting green energy and supply chain disruptions. A bilateral tariff framework extends through November 10, 2026.

**Geopolitical significance:**
China's agricultural import shift is not tactical — it is structural. US farmers have permanently lost market share that will not return even if tariffs are removed. This reflects a broader Chinese strategy of supply chain decoupling from the US across all critical inputs. China is simultaneously accelerating its own domestic chip production, EV supply chains, and food security infrastructure. The interdependence that was supposed to make conflict too costly is being systematically dismantled — by both sides.

**Investment implications:**
- **Brazilian agriculture/agribusiness (strongly bullish):** Brazil is the primary beneficiary of China's US agricultural exit. Brazilian soy, corn, and protein exporters. BRL exposure attractive on a multi-year horizon.
- **US agricultural commodities (bearish long-term):** Structural demand destruction from China. Corn and soy farmers face a permanent demand hole.
- **China domestic tech/semiconductor (bullish in China context):** Policy-driven acceleration of domestic substitution. Not easily accessible for foreign investors, but affects global semiconductor pricing dynamics.
- **USD/CNH (stable-to-bearish USD):** China maintaining yuan stability (~7.0) as a policy tool. Dollar weakness trend (-13.5% vs EUR since Sep 2025) reflects broader erosion of US safe-haven premium.

## Event 4: Ukraine–Russia — Peace Process Frozen, Military Stalemate Deepens

**What happened:**
US-brokered ceasefire negotiations between Russia and Ukraine were put on hold in early March 2026, as the Iran conflict consumed US diplomatic bandwidth. France and the UK are pledging to deploy military "hubs" inside Ukraine as part of a prospective peacekeeping framework. Russia continues to reject NATO troop presence on Ukrainian soil under any conditions. The fourth anniversary of the invasion passed in February with a UN resolution demanding ceasefire — ignored by Moscow.

**Geopolitical significance:**
The peace process is not dead but indefinitely deferred. The US's strategic attention has shifted to the Middle East, leaving Europe to manage the Ukraine file with less American leadership than at any point since 2022. This accelerates European strategic autonomy — both in defense spending and in diplomatic positioning. France and the UK acting as security guarantors without US leadership is a structural shift in Atlantic security architecture that markets have not fully priced.

**Investment implications:**
- **European defense (strongly bullish):** France, Germany, Poland, and Nordic countries all accelerating procurement. Multi-year spending tailwind. European defense ETFs and prime contractors (Rheinmetall, Thales, BAE Systems) remain high-conviction.
- **Ukrainian reconstruction (speculative/long-term):** If and when peace is reached, Ukrainian infrastructure reconstruction represents one of the largest investment opportunities in European history. Premature now, but positioning for this is not.
- **Eastern European equities (mixed):** Poland and Baltic states benefit from NATO spending; exposure to conflict tail-risk remains.
- **Natural gas (watch carefully):** Any peace framework affects Russian gas flows to Europe. A ceasefire could depress European gas prices. A collapse could spike them again.

## Event 5: Dollar Weakness — The Safe-Haven Premium is Eroding

**What happened:**
The US dollar has depreciated 13.5% against the euro and 13.9% against the Swiss franc since September 2025. EUR/USD is trading at ~1.16 and analysts see a path to 1.20 over 12 months. Markets are pricing an increased probability of US recession, driven by tariff-induced stagflation, Iran oil shock impact, and political pressure on Fed independence (concern about Trump attempting to influence rate policy ahead of midterms).

**Geopolitical significance:**
Dollar weakness is not just a currency story — it is a signal about US hegemonic credibility. The dollar's reserve currency status rests partly on institutional confidence in US policy predictability. When tariffs, active wars, and political interference with the Fed all occur simultaneously, the confidence premium compresses. The euro and yuan are both pressing their global ambitions into this gap. This is a slow process measured in years, but the direction is now unambiguous.

**Investment implications:**
- **Gold (strongly bullish):** Classic hedge against dollar debasement + geopolitical uncertainty + stagflation. Gold is performing its historical function.
- **Non-US developed market equities (bullish for USD-based investors):** EUR appreciation amplifies returns on European equity held in USD terms.
- **EM currencies with strong fundamentals (selective bullish):** BRL, INR, IDR benefit from dollar weakness and commodity tailwinds. Differentiate from EM currencies exposed to US tariffs or Iran oil shock.
- **US large-cap multinationals (bearish on FX translation):** Dollar weakness hurts foreign earnings reported in USD. Underweight US multinationals with high foreign revenue exposure in a weaker dollar environment — this is counterintuitive but correct.
