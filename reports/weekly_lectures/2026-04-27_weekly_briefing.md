## Event 1: Iran Re-Closes the Strait of Hormuz — Ceasefire Collapse

### What Happened
The two-week US-Iran-Israel ceasefire agreed April 7–8 has broken down. Iran reimposed restrictions on Strait of Hormuz shipping on April 18, citing "repeated breaches of trust" by the United States. By April 22, Iran had seized vessels transiting the strait. As of April 26, Tehran has formally rejected further talks and the White House has cancelled its envoy's trip to the region. The US military is now developing active targeting plans for Iran's Hormuz asymmetric capabilities — fast attack boats, mine-layers, and coastal defense systems.

The Strait currently carries roughly 20 mb/d of oil under normal conditions. Actual throughput has collapsed to approximately 3.8 mb/d, a reduction of more than 80%.

### Geopolitical Significance
This is the most consequential maritime chokepoint event since the Suez Crisis of 1956. Iran's willingness to reimpose restrictions even after a ceasefire signals that this is not a tactical maneuver but a structural posture — the Islamic Republic is betting that energy price pain will fracture Western coalition support. The US military now faces a genuine escalation decision: accept the blockade (economically damaging) or strike Hormuz defenses (risks full war). There is no clean option.

Russia and China vetoed a UN Security Council resolution on April 7 that would have authorized safe passage; both are benefiting from the disorder and have no interest in resolution.

### Investment Implications
- **Crude oil (Brent/WTI): LONG structural.** Supply disruption of this magnitude — 9.1 mb/d of Gulf shut-ins projected for April vs. 7.5 mb/d in March — supports $90–110/bbl range as base case. The question is no longer "will oil stay high" but "how high if the US strikes Iran."
- **LNG exporters (US, Qatar, Australia): STRONG LONG.** Asian buyers are scrambling to redirect supply. US LNG export terminals (Cheniere, NextDecade) and Australian LNG operators benefit as spot LNG prices spike. This is the 6–18 month structural trade.
- **Defense contractors (RTX, LMT, NOC, BA): LONG.** Active military planning around Hormuz is an explicit demand catalyst. Gulf state defense budgets were already expanding; this accelerates procurement.
- **Eurozone equities: CAUTIOUS/SHORT consumer discretionary.** The Eurozone private sector contracted in April at the fastest pace since November 2024 as the Iran war drove up energy costs. EUR/USD weakened below $1.17. Energy import costs are crushing margins.
- **Shipping/tanker rates: HIGHLY VOLATILE.** War-risk premiums make this a trading instrument, not an investment instrument. Avoid unless you have very specific operational knowledge.

## Event 2: US Treasury Sanctions China-Iran Oil Network — New Front Opens

### What Happened
On April 24, the US Treasury Department imposed sweeping sanctions on 40 shipping companies, vessels, and a Chinese oil refinery specifically targeting what Treasury called "secretive oil and chemical trade between Beijing and Tehran." This is the most aggressive direct targeting of Chinese entities for Iran sanctions violations to date. It follows President Trump's April 13 threat to impose 50% tariffs on China following unverified reports that Beijing was preparing to ship air defense systems to Iran.

Simultaneously, the USTR launched two new Section 301 investigations into Chinese industrial overcapacity and forced labor, with hearings scheduled for April–May. If findings require action, additional tariffs on Chinese goods are live.

Current average US tariffs on Chinese exports: **47.5%**, covering 100% of Chinese goods. Current Chinese counter-tariffs on US goods: **31.9%**.

### Geopolitical Significance
Washington is explicitly weaponizing the Iran conflict to press China on its economic relationship with Tehran. Beijing is in a structurally difficult position: it needs Iranian oil (cheap, sanctions-discounted supply), but cannot absorb further US tariff escalation. China's new State Council supply chain security regulations (April 7) — which expand government powers to counteract foreign entities threatening Chinese industrial supply chains — read as a pre-positioning move. Beijing knows further US sanctions are coming and is building the legal architecture to retaliate.

The Russia-China-Iran axis is operating as a de facto economic bloc, and the US is now applying systematic pressure across all three nodes simultaneously.

### Investment Implications
- **Chinese export-facing equities: UNDERWEIGHT.** 47.5% tariff is effectively trade wall. Additional tariffs would be devastating for margins in electronics, machinery, and consumer goods. Avoid Chinese ADRs with significant US revenue exposure.
- **Chinese domestic consumption names: HOLD/SELECTIVE.** PBoC fixing yuan 300–500 pips weaker than market projections is deliberate — Beijing is using currency depreciation as a partial tariff offset. Offshore yuan (CNH) likely weakens further in near-term.
- **Gold: LONG.** US-China economic warfare + Middle East conflict = maximum geopolitical uncertainty. Gold is the canonical beneficiary. The structural case for $2,800–3,200+ range remains intact.
- **US semiconductor/defense supply chain: LONG.** Every round of China sanctions accelerates reshoring and allied diversification of critical supply chains. Taiwan Strait risk premium is embedded, not priced.

## Event 3: IMF Issues Recession Warning — "Global Economy in the Shadow of War"

### What Happened
The IMF's April 2026 World Economic Outlook, published April 14–15, revised global growth down to **3.1%** for 2026 (from prior estimates above 3.5%). The IMF explicitly warned that an adverse conflict scenario pushes growth to 2.5% and inflation to 5.4%, while a severe scenario brings global growth to **2.0%** — the threshold historically defined as a near-global recession. This would be only the fifth time since 1980.

The IMF highlighted the Hormuz crisis as the primary macro shock but also noted reinvigorated trade fragmentation and AI productivity uncertainty as compounding factors.

### Geopolitical Significance
The IMF's framing — "Global Economy Tested Again" — is a direct echo of 2022's post-invasion language. The institution is signaling that the current crisis is institutionally comparable to the 2022 shock in severity. Crucially, this is a supply-side shock, not a demand-side shock: energy price inflation is being transmitted into broader goods inflation, which limits central bank room to cut rates in response to slowing growth. This is stagflation risk, not standard recession risk.

### Investment Implications
- **Fixed income / sovereign bonds: CAUTIOUS.** Central banks cannot cut into an oil-driven inflation spike without credibility damage. Expect Fed to hold, ECB to hold. Short duration bias appropriate.
- **TIPS / inflation linkers: LONG.** If stagflation scenario materializes, inflation protection is underpriced at current levels.
- **EM equities (energy exporters): SELECT LONG.** Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait GCC markets benefit from high oil prices offsetting global growth drag. Indonesia and Malaysia (LNG) also benefit.
- **EM equities (energy importers): UNDERWEIGHT.** India, South Korea, Japan face energy cost drag. Particularly vulnerable: Indian current account deficit widens with oil at $100+.

## Event 4: Ukraine Strikes Russian Oil Infrastructure — Russia Threatens Europe

### What Happened
On April 26, Ukrainian drones attacked an oil refinery in Yaroslavl, Russia, starting a significant fire. This follows two weeks of sustained Ukrainian long-range drone strikes targeting Russian oil and gas infrastructure. Russia has issued a formal warning to European countries and industries against funding Ukraine's long-range drone production, threatening unspecified retaliation against "economic infrastructure targets" in Europe.

As of April 26, Russia had suffered approximately 149 combat engagements in the past 24 hours, and cumulative Russian combat personnel losses since February 2022 are estimated at approximately 1.33 million.

### Geopolitical Significance
Ukraine has discovered that targeting Russian energy infrastructure serves a dual purpose: it degrades Russian revenue (oil refinery capacity) and it demonstrates to European capitals that they can impose tangible economic costs on Russia, strengthening the political case for continued support. Russia's threat against European "economic infrastructure" is a significant escalation signal — this language has not been used this explicitly before. The question is whether it translates into action (sabotage of Baltic undersea infrastructure, power grids) or remains deterrence posture.

European energy security is now under pressure from two directions simultaneously: Iranian oil disruption from the south, Russian threats from the east.

### Investment Implications
- **European natural gas (TTF): LONG volatility.** Russian threats create a risk premium floor regardless of actual delivery. Physical supply is constrained by Hormuz.
- **Nuclear energy equities/uranium: STRONG LONG.** Europe's strategic argument for domestic energy — meaning nuclear — is being validated in real time. Cameco, NexGen, and European nuclear operators benefit. This is an 18–36 month structural position, not a trade.
- **Baltic/Eastern European defense spending: LONG.** Poland, the Baltic states, and Germany are all accelerating defense procurement. European defense ETFs (EUAD, DFEN) benefit.
- **Underweight German and Italian industrials.** Energy-intensive manufacturing faces margin compression from dual supply shock.

## Event 5: US-China Trade War Escalates — New Tariff Threats, Section 301 Investigations

### What Happened
Beyond the Iran-linked sanctions (Event 2), the underlying US-China trade architecture is actively deteriorating. On April 7, China's State Council issued new industrial supply chain security regulations expanding Beijing's power to impose countermeasures on foreign entities. On April 13, Trump threatened 50% tariffs linked to alleged Chinese arms transfers to Iran (unverified). USTR launched two Section 301 investigations with April–May hearings. With base tariffs already at 47.5%, markets are beginning to price in a structural trade-wall scenario.

The global supply chain stress index is elevated, with shipping delays well above historical norms.

### Geopolitical Significance
The US-China economic relationship is undergoing structural bifurcation. This is not a negotiating tactic — it is a multi-year strategic decoupling in advanced manufacturing, semiconductors, and now energy supply chains. Both countries are building parallel economic architectures, and the Iran crisis is accelerating the timeline. The critical question for 2026H2: does China's economic pressure from tariffs + Iran sanctions force a strategic adjustment (accommodating the US) or a strategic hardening (deepening China-Russia-Iran axis)?

Current signals point to hardening.

### Investment Implications
- **Vietnam, India, Mexico (supply chain relocation beneficiaries): LONG.** Every round of China-US escalation accelerates friend-shoring. Vietnam semiconductor and electronics manufacturing; India services and pharma; Mexico nearshoring in automotive and electronics.
- **US domestic industrials / reshoring plays: LONG.** Companies with domestic US production benefit from tariff walls. Industrial CAPEX into the US continues.
- **Taiwan Strait risk premium: MONITOR.** If China hardens on the US-Iran conflict, Taiwan risk perceptions will follow. This is the tail risk that changes everything. No position, but it should be the first scenario in your risk framework.
