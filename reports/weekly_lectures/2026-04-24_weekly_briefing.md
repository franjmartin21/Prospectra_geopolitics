## Event 1: Hormuz Crisis — Ceasefire Collapses, Iran Re-seizes Ships

### What Happened
The April 8 ceasefire between the US and Iran, which had briefly appeared to open the Strait of Hormuz, has effectively collapsed. Iran's Foreign Minister declared the Strait "fully open" on April 17 — crude fell 10% on the announcement — but within hours Iran had reimposed controls, opened fire on tankers, and seized two container ships attempting to transit "without authorization." Iran's parliament speaker Ghalibaf, who had been on the negotiating team, subsequently resigned and declared publicly: *"Reopening the Strait of Hormuz is impossible"* as long as the US blockade of Iranian ports remains in place. The US and Iran are now in an acknowledged tit-for-tat loop: Iran controls the Strait; the US blockades Iranian ports. Traffic through the strait — normally 100+ ships daily — remains far below pre-war levels.

### Geopolitical Significance
This is no longer a crisis that could resolve quickly. Both sides have locked themselves into mutually coercive positions. The US cannot simply lift the port blockade without appearing to capitulate to Iranian pressure; Iran cannot reopen the Strait without signaling defeat to its domestic audience. The ceasefire negotiations are not dead but are structurally broken — the gap between what each side can publicly accept is larger than the gap between what each side privately wants. Meanwhile, Iran has demonstrated it can use commercial shipping as leverage without firing a shot at US military assets, which changes the escalation calculus. Watch for: Israeli escalation (previously threatened strikes on Iranian energy infrastructure), which would remove the ceasefire framework entirely.

**OPEC+ Catalyst — May 3:** The group agreed to a symbolic 206,000 bbl/day quota increase for May, but the key members whose barrels are actually blocked (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, Iraq) cannot physically increase exports while the Strait is closed. The May 3 meeting is the real signal to watch — any language about production restoration timelines implies a ceasefire timeline.

## Event 2: US-China Trade War — China Arms Shipment Triggers 50% Tariff Threat

### What Happened
On April 13, reports emerged that China was preparing a weapons shipment to Iran. Trump responded with a threat to impose 50% additional tariffs on China — on top of the existing 145% tariff structure (China retaliating with 125%). The threat has not yet been formalized, but the strategic logic is now explicit: tariffs are no longer just economic tools, they are foreign policy instruments tied to the Iran conflict. Separately, the Supreme Court's February 2026 ruling striking down IEEPA-based tariffs has forced the administration to pivot to Section 301 and Section 232 frameworks — a more legally durable but slower mechanism. A US-China Summit is reportedly still scheduled for May.

### Geopolitical Significance
The China-Iran arms question is the single most dangerous potential escalation in the current environment. If China supplies weapons — or is credibly accused of doing so — the US-China trade conflict transforms from an economic dispute into a proxy war dynamic. The May Summit is now a hostage to the arms shipment narrative. This is also notable for what it reveals about China's strategic calculus: Beijing is using its relationship with Iran to apply pressure on the Hormuz situation, which it needs resolved for its own energy supply security (China receives ~15% of its crude through Hormuz). This is not a clean play for Beijing — it's managed chaos.

## Event 5: Copper Hits Structural Inflection — Deficit Confirmed, AI Demand Adds Layer

### What Happened
Copper hit $14,527/mt on the LME in January 2026 — a new all-time record — and has since consolidated around $12,500-13,300/mt. The structural supply-demand picture has clarified: JPMorgan projects a 330,000 MT refined copper deficit for 2026; the International Copper Study Group (conservative) projects 150,000 MT deficit. The IEA confirmed that 25% of global mine output — approximately 6.4 million tonnes — is currently stalled or suspended due to ESG issues. New data point this week: the AI infrastructure buildout (data centers, cloud compute) has been confirmed as a new primary demand driver, rivaling traditional electrification demand. Project lead times for new copper supply: 17 years.

### Geopolitical Significance
Copper is where the geopolitical investment thesis concentrates: every major structural theme in our portfolio — energy transition, rearmament, semiconductor manufacturing, AI infrastructure, EV electrification — is copper-intensive. The supply side is controlled by politically unstable jurisdictions (Chile, Peru have both had resource nationalism episodes in the past 24 months). The demand side is driven by policy mandates that are legally locked in across G7 economies. This is the most concentrated single-commodity expression of the multipolar transition thesis. The 17-year project lead time means no supply response is possible before 2043 for projects beginning today.
