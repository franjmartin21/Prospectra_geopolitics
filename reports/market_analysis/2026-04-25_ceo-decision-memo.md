## Decision 2: Copper Bear Case Stress-Test

**Position opened:** April 24, 2026 (yesterday)
**Instruments:** COPX ~60% / SCCO ~25% / FCX ~15%
**Close rule:** Three consecutive monthly closes below $9,500/mt
**Current price:** ~$12,500–13,300/mt (ATH: $14,527 in January 2026)

### The Bear Case (Goldman Sachs Logic — Steel-Manned)

Goldman has been episodically bearish on copper at record prices. Their argument is coherent and deserves honest engagement:

**Demand destruction:** At $12,500–14,000/mt, manufacturers substitute, redesign, and reduce copper content. Automotive engineers spec aluminum where copper is not essential. This has happened in every prior commodity supercycle. Price is the best cure for high prices.

**China property collapse:** 30–40% of copper demand is construction and real estate. China's property sector has been in distress for three years. If the structural slowdown in Chinese construction is more severe than consensus assumes — and it may be — then demand projections built on "China recovery" are wrong.

**EV demand timeline slippage:** Every copper demand model assumes accelerating EV penetration. But EV adoption timelines have slipped repeatedly (charging infrastructure, grid capacity, consumer preference). If EVs are 3–4 years behind current projections, the demand surge is delayed, not cancelled — but delayed enough to matter for a 12–24 month position.

**Supply response:** The 17-year median lead time is real, but it describes a *median*, not a law. Short-cycle supply responses (reopening suspended mines, accelerating existing expansions) can add 500,000–1M tonnes in 2–3 years if prices justify it. The 25% of output "suspended on ESG issues" is not all permanently offline.

**Recession tail risk:** Copper is highly correlated with global industrial production. A US or global recession — not the base case but not zero probability — causes demand destruction that overwhelms supply constraints, at least temporarily.

### The Bull Case Rebuttal

| Bear Argument | Bull Counter |
|---|---|
| Demand destruction at high prices | Transition demand is policy-mandated — not discretionary. Utilities cannot substitute copper in transmission infrastructure. |
| China property collapse | AI data center demand is *new* demand the bear case didn't model. JPMorgan and IEA now confirm this as a primary driver rivaling electrification. |
| EV timeline slippage | Rearmament demand (European defense boom, which we hold separately) is an independent copper demand driver not in any commodity bull/bear model from 2024. |
| Supply response | The 25% of suspended output is suspended on ESG grounds, resource nationalism, and permitting — structural constraints that do not lift when prices rise. ESG constraints are tightening, not loosening. |
| Recession risk | This is the real risk. It is correctly specified as the wrong-if condition. A global recession causing demand destruction IS the scenario under which we close. |

### CEO Verdict: **Position Confirmed — Bear Case Does Not Override**

The bear case is intellectually serious and Bolo should know it cold before executing any copper allocation. But it requires a conjunction of events — global recession AND China demand miss AND EV slippage AND supply response acceleration — that is not the base case under any current macro projection.

The bear case's strongest leg is **China property** — this is real and not fully resolved. But AI data center demand is a genuinely new demand driver that was not in any 2024 copper model and has been confirmed this week by the IEA as a primary demand category. That new information changes the demand floor calculation.

**Formal stress-test outcome: Thesis survives. Position stays open. Conviction: HIGH.**

The one thing I'm watching: if China announces any significant property stimulus package that is credibly likely to restore construction demand, I want to reassess whether the recovery in Chinese construction demand competes with transition demand in a way that changes the supply-demand dynamic. Short answer: it doesn't — more Chinese construction = more copper demand, not less — but the framing matters.
